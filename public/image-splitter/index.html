Split an image into a grid or carousel

Drop a photo and it's split instantly into a 3×3 grid — no clicks needed. Switch to Carousel for equal panorama slices, dial in your own rows and columns, or tap the Instagram preset. Every piece exports at full resolution, no upload, no quality loss.

How to split an image

  1. Add your photo. Drag & drop it onto the tool, click to browse, or paste from your clipboard. It's split into a 3×3 grid the instant it loads.
  2. Pick a mode. Stay in Grid and set your own rows and columns (or tap the Instagram 3-column preset), or switch to Carousel for equal vertical slices.
  3. Check the preview. Grid mode shows the pieces laid out with gaps, the way they'll actually look once posted.
  4. Download. Grab individual pieces or everything at once as a ZIP — full original resolution, no watermark.

What people use it for

  • Instagram grid (puzzle) posts — split one photo into a 3×N grid that reassembles on your profile.
  • Carousel / panorama posts — slice a wide photo into equal panels that swipe together as one image.
  • TikTok banners — cut a single graphic into the strips TikTok's profile layout expects.
  • Tiling large images — break a big photo or graphic into smaller pieces for printing or sharing.

Frequently asked questions

Does splitting an image reduce its quality?

No. Each piece is cropped straight from the original pixels with no resizing or recompression — a PNG export is pixel-for-pixel identical to the matching region of the source photo. JPEG export uses a high quality setting.

Is my photo uploaded to a server?

No. Splitting runs locally in your browser using Canvas. Your photo never leaves your device — you can confirm it yourself in your browser's DevTools Network tab.

Is this tool free?

Yes — completely free, no sign-up, no watermark, and no limits.

How many pieces can I split into?

Grid mode goes up to 10 columns and 10 rows (100 pieces). Carousel mode supports 2 to 10 equal vertical slices.

What image formats are supported?

Load PNG, JPEG, or WebP images, and export the pieces as PNG or JPEG at the original resolution — one at a time or all together in a ZIP.
